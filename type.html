Types of Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

No Diabetic Retinopathy (No DR)

Description: In the "No DR" stage, there are no signs of diabetic retinopathy. The retina is healthy, and there is no evidence of damage.

Causes: The absence of high blood sugar levels causing damage to the blood vessels in the retina.

Mild Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (Mild DR)

Description: The earliest stage of DR, where small changes in the blood vessels of the retina occur. These may include tiny aneurysms and microaneurysms.

Causes: High blood sugar starts to weaken the retina's blood vessels, leading to leakage of fluid and small areas of bleeding.

Moderate Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (Moderate DR)

Description: At this stage, changes in the retina become more noticeable. Blood vessels become blocked or more enlarged.

Causes: Ongoing blood sugar elevation causes more damage to the retinal blood vessels, leading to areas with restricted blood flow.

Severe Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (Severe DR)

Description: Significant damage occurs, with many blood vessels blocked and new, abnormal blood vessels starting to grow.

Causes: Ongoing retinal vascular damage causes reduced blood supply, which can lead to vision problems and severe complications.

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (Proliferative DR)

Description: The most advanced stage of DR, where abnormal blood vessels grow on the retina or optic nerve. These new vessels are fragile and prone to bleeding.

Causes: Chronic high blood sugar can cause extensive retinal damage, leading to new blood vessels that can lead to retinal detachment, blindness, or severe vision loss.
